Analyzing Retention — guiding product through listener behavior

A deep-dive product analysis evaluating Pandora listener engagement, culminating in a new behavioral retention metric.

▶ Overview

A growing user base with a cyclical engagement problem

Overall monthly unique listeners were steadily growing (+3.31% over three years), but cyclical engagement patterns suggested that new users were losing interest over time.

Crucially, retention varied substantially by platform. iOS and Roku were gaining Monthly Uniques (MUs), while Android and Web were actively losing them. Because new listener acquisition rates did not predict this retention, the discrepancies pointed to deeper, platform-specific behavioral issues.

Project goal

Investigate the underlying causes of listener loss across platforms, evaluate the efficacy of current engagement metrics, and propose data-backed UX recommendations.

Research questions

Why do retention rates vary so widely between platforms? Are we tracking the right metric to predict long-term satisfaction and active product engagement?

⏸ Methodology

A new North Star for retention

The existing standard—Average listening time per user—was flawed. Extended listening is often a passive behavior (e.g., leaving the app playing in the background), making it difficult to gauge true user satisfaction.

📊

The New Metric

Average Active Days per Monthly Unique. Calculated by dividing the sum of daily unique listeners by the number of unique listeners for that month.

🧠

The Rationale

It measures purposeful behavior. Choosing to open the app on multiple distinct days indicates active decision-making, satisfaction with the product, and a desire to return.

⚖️

The Benefit

It is highly intuitive, easy to calculate, and perfectly comparative—allowing us to cleanly evaluate engagement across entirely different platforms and time points.

⏺ Key Findings

Two distinct listener archetypes

Applying the "Average Active Days" metric revealed a massive gap between mobile and connected-home/web platforms. Mobile users engaged frequently but for short durations, while web/connected users engaged infrequently but for long durations.

Mobile Behavior

High active days (~10 days/mo) but the shortest average session lengths. Fast, frequent, active use.

Web & Connected

Low active days (~5 days/mo) but highly extended continuous listening time. Infrequent, passive use.

The Android Problem

Android users shared the exact same behavioral archetype as iOS, yet Android was losing overall MUs.

YoY Growth

Between 2019 and 2020, Roku (+17%) and Amazon (+10%) were the only platforms to show growth in listening hours.

Average Active Days per Monthly Unique (Dec 2019)

The gap between mobile (iOS/Android) and other platforms is substantial, suggesting fundamental behavioral differences in how the product is utilized.

⏹ Recommendations & Impact

Strategic UX & Product Priorities

  • Investigate Android UX: With behavior mirroring iOS but retention dropping, conduct a targeted audit of the Android interface for performance bottlenecks or usability friction.
  • Analyze the Web On-Ramp: Web is gaining users fastest but also losing them fastest. Determine if it serves as a temporary discovery portal or if UX issues are driving abandonment.
  • Boost Connected Engagement: Explore push reminders, cross-device continuations, and in-vehicle integrations to increase the active days of Roku/Amazon users.
  • Optimize Ad Delivery: Adapt monetization models to platform behavior—front-load ads on mobile (frequent/short sessions) and space them out on Web/Roku (infrequent/long sessions).
